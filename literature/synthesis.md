## Executive summary (read this first)

- **The physical chain is well established.** Warming raises vapor pressure deficit (VPD), which dries live and dead fuels; drier fuels burn more readily and spread faster. The VPD→area-burned relationship is strongly *non-linear (roughly exponential)*, so modest warming yields outsized increases in fire potential (Abatzoglou & Williams 2016; Williams et al. 2019). Temperature is therefore an *integrated* index of hot–dry fire weather — it is **correlated with, not independent of, moisture** — which matters for how any temperature↔area-burned correlation is interpreted (see §1).

- **Canada is warming ~2× the global rate (northern Canada ~3×)** (Canada's Changing Climate Report 2019). **[VERIFIED]** Area burned and fire-season length have trended upward over the last half-century, though with strong regional heterogeneity (Hanes et al. 2019).

- **2023 was a genuine outlier: ~15–18 Mha burned, ~2.5× the previous record** (Wikipedia/NRCan; WWA 2023). **[VERIFIED]** World Weather Attribution found climate change made the season's *cumulative fire-weather severity* **≥7× more likely and ~50% more intense**, and *peak* fire weather **≥2× more likely (~20% more intense)** (WWA 2023). **[VERIFIED]**

- **The 2023 attribution papers are real.** Byrne et al. 2024 (*Nature*, "Unprecedented Canadian forest fire carbon emissions during 2023") and Jones et al. 2024 (*ESSD*, "State of Wildfires 2023–2024") are confirmed citable sources. **[VERIFIED]** A separate **Jain et al. 2024** Canada-2023 drivers paper is real to the best of my knowledge (*Nature Communications*) but **I could not fetch-verify its DOI/abstract this session — confirm before citing a specific number to it. [UNVERIFIED]**

- **2025 was the second-largest season on record (~8.9 Mha), behind only 2023** (Wikipedia; CIFFC). **[VERIFIED]**

- **The single most important caveat for public framing:** the defensible claim is **rate-of-change and regional exceedance of the Holocene range**, *not* an absolute "the most the boreal has ever burned." Charcoal reconstructions (Marlon, Power, Kelly, Girardin, Ali, Bergeron et al.) show (i) some regions (Alaskan boreal) now burning at rates unprecedented in ~10,000 years (Kelly et al. 2013), *but also* (ii) that parts of the **eastern** Canadian boreal have *declined* in fire over recent centuries, and (iii) a 20th-century "fire deficit" from suppression complicates "unprecedented" claims. Charcoal is a proxy and **not** directly comparable to modern hectare counts (see §3).

- **Area burned ≠ air-quality crisis.** Rising area burned is climate-linked. But whether smoke chokes a given city is a **meteorology/transport** phenomenon (fire location, plume injection height, wind trajectory, population downwind) — not a simple function of total hectares (see §4).

## 1. The mechanisms — how climate change raises fire risk

**Temperature → VPD → fuel moisture.** Vapor pressure deficit (VPD) is the difference between how much water vapor the air can hold at a given temperature and how much it actually holds; because saturation vapor pressure rises roughly exponentially with temperature (Clausius–Clapeyron, ~7%/°C), warmer air is "thirstier." Higher VPD pulls moisture out of live vegetation and, critically, out of **dead fuels** (litter, downed wood, duff), lowering their moisture content and raising flammability (Seager et al. 2015, *J. Appl. Meteorol. Climatol.* 54:1121–1141 — VPD is "an important fire-related meteorological quantity," and VPD "correlates strongly with the area … burned" [VERIFIED via Wikipedia VPD page]).

**The relationship is non-linear (~exponential).** Fire activity responds steeply to fuel aridity: below a moisture threshold little burns; above it, area burned climbs sharply. Abatzoglou & Williams (2016, *PNAS* 113:11770–11775, "Impact of anthropogenic climate change on wildfire across western US forests") showed anthropogenic warming roughly *doubled* western-US forest area burned 1984–2015 via fuel-aridity (VPD) increases; Williams et al. (2019, *Earth's Future*) extended this, emphasizing the exponential VPD–burned-area link. **[UNVERIFIED specific numbers — confirm; established finding.]** The practical upshot: a small rise in mean temperature produces a disproportionately large rise in fire *potential*.

**Drought and antecedent precipitation.** Seasonal-to-multiyear moisture deficits precondition landscapes. Low winter snowpack and dry springs (as in 2023) mean fuels are primed early. In the Canadian system this is captured operationally by the **Canadian Forest Fire Danger Rating System (CFFDRS)** and its **Fire Weather Index (FWI)** — a suite of moisture codes (Fine Fuel Moisture Code, Duff Moisture Code, Drought Code) and behavior indices (ISI, BUI, FWI) driven by temperature, humidity, wind, and rain (Van Wagner 1987; Wotton 2009). FWI is *the* operational bridge between weather/climate and fire danger, which is why attribution studies analyze FWI rather than hectares (WWA 2023 explicitly used fire-weather indices "rather than … fire regime variables such as area burned" to isolate the climate signal [VERIFIED]).

**Overwinter Drought Code and deep organic (duff/peat) drying.** The **Drought Code (DC)** tracks moisture in deep, compact organic layers with a very long drying timescale (weeks–months). Boreal soils are largely organic (peat, duff); when the DC stays high through a dry autumn and a low-snow winter, these deep layers enter spring already dry, enabling **smoldering combustion** that consumes carbon-rich soil and is hard to extinguish.

**Overwintering "holdover / zombie" fires.** Smoldering fires can persist underground beneath snow through winter and re-emerge in spring ("holdover" / "overwintering" / "zombie" fires) (Scholten et al. 2021, *Nature*, "Overwintering fires in boreal forests"). These are favored by hot summers and deep burning. Quantitatively they are a **small** share of total area/emissions in a typical multi-year window (on the order of <1% of area burned across Alaska + NWT 2002–2018 [VERIFIED via Wikipedia holdover-fire page, which cites a 2002–2018 study: "burned only 0.8% of the total area … 0.5% of … carbon emissions"]) but can spike after extreme fire years. **[Scholten et al. 2021 exact fractions UNVERIFIED — confirm.]**

**Lightning ignition trends.** Most *large* boreal burned area comes from lightning-ignited fires (often in remote areas where suppression is delayed). Warming is expected to increase convective activity and lightning at high latitudes, raising ignition frequency (Veraverbeke et al. 2017, *Nature Climate Change*, "Lightning as a major driver of recent large fire years in North American boreal forests"). **[UNVERIFIED specifics — established finding.]**

**Fire-season length.** The window of burnable conditions has lengthened — snow-free season starts earlier and ends later. Hanes et al. (2019, *Can. J. For. Res.* 49:256–269, "Fire-regime changes in Canada over the last half century") document increasing fire-season length and shifting regional fire regimes across Canada over ~1959–2015. **[Title/journal correct to my knowledge; abstract numbers UNVERIFIED this session — publisher blocked fetch. Confirm specific figures.]**

**Atmospheric blocking / heat domes.** Persistent high-pressure ridges (blocking) create hot, dry, sunny, subsiding conditions that spike VPD and suppress precipitation over large areas — the synoptic setup behind many extreme fire spells (e.g., the 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome; ridging over Canada in 2023). Whether the *frequency/persistence* of such blocking is itself changing with climate is an area of active, less-settled research.

**Why temperature is an *integrated* fire-weather indicator (interpretation caveat).** Because VPD, evaporative demand, snowmelt timing, and fuel drying all rise with temperature, **temperature acts as a proxy that bundles the hot-and-dry conditions that drive fire.** A statistical correlation between summer temperature and area burned is therefore **expected and physically grounded — but temperature and moisture are not independent predictors.** One should not treat a temperature↔area-burned regression as if temperature were acting on fire through some channel separate from aridity; much of temperature's apparent "effect" *is* the moisture effect (via VPD and fuel drying). This argues for framing temperature as an index of integrated fire-weather forcing, and for caution against double-counting temperature and moisture, or over-interpreting the slope of a simple temperature–area regression as a mechanism-free causal coefficient.

## 2. Attribution of recent extreme seasons

**2023 — the record season.**
- **Scale:** ~15 Mha (NRCan/ECCC framing; "over 15 million hectares … more than seven times the 40-year average" [VERIFIED via Wikipedia *Climate change in Canada*]) to ~18.5 Mha (final CIFFC tally, ~18,544,708 ha [VERIFIED via Wikipedia *2023 Canadian wildfires* monthly total]). The season was **"more than 2.5 times the previous record"** and "more than twice that of any year since 1983" [VERIFIED]. Different totals reflect different cut-off dates; **~15 Mha is a conservative headline; ~18 Mha is the full-year CIFFC total.**
- **Human cost:** ~185,000–232,000 evacuated; 8 wildfire-personnel deaths [VERIFIED]. (A 2025 *Nature* study later attributed thousands of acute and tens of thousands of chronic PM2.5-related deaths across North America/Europe to the smoke [VERIFIED via Wikipedia; treat as a modeled epidemiological estimate].)

- **World Weather Attribution (WWA), 22 Aug 2023, "Climate change more than doubled the likelihood of extreme fire weather conditions in eastern Canada" (DOI 10.25561/105981):** **[VERIFIED — fetched directly]**
  - Cumulative severity (seasonal DSR): climate change made a season this severe **"at least seven times more likely"** and **"around 50% more intense."**
  - Peak fire weather (FWI7x): **"at least twice as likely,"** intensity **"increased by about 20%."**
  - In today's climate such fire weather is a roughly **once-in-20–25-year** event (~4–5%/yr).
  - Methodological note WWA itself flags: **"Observed changes are typically larger than in the models,"** i.e., their ratios may be conservative; and risk is "projected to increase further in a 2°C warmer world."

- **Jones et al. 2024, "State of Wildfires 2023–2024," *Earth System Science Data* 16:3601–3685 (DOI 10.5194/essd-16-3601-2024):** **[VERIFIED — fetched directly]** Global review; Canada is a focal event. For Canada 2023: burned area **~6× the average**, fire carbon emissions **>9× the 2003+ average**, contributing **24% of global fire C emissions** (vs ~3% typically). Modeled burned-area anomaly **up to ~40% higher due to climate change**; likelihood of high fire weather increased **~2.9–3.6-fold**; Canada-magnitude events projected **6.3–10.8× more frequent** under SSP3-7.0 by 2100. (Note: this paper *cites* a "Jain et al., 2024" for evacuation figures — evidence that a distinct Jain 2024 Canada paper exists — but Jones et al. is a different, global paper.)

- **Byrne et al. 2024, "Unprecedented Canadian forest fire carbon emissions during 2023," *Nature* (published 28 Aug 2024):** **[VERIFIED that the paper exists — confirmed via NASA/JPL & ResearchSquare preprint; publisher fetch blocked]** Finds 2023 Canadian fire carbon emissions were an extreme outlier, comparable to or exceeding the annual fossil-fuel emissions of major nations, driven by record warmth/dryness. **[Exact Tg C figure UNVERIFIED this session — a related Chen et al. 2026 GCB estimate put 2023 Canada at ~574 Tg C; confirm Byrne's own number before quoting.]**

- **Jain et al. 2024 (2023 Canada drivers/impacts), likely *Nature Communications*:** To the best of my knowledge this is a real paper ("Drivers and impacts of the record-breaking 2023 wildfire season in Canada" or similar). **[UNVERIFIED — could not fetch DOI/abstract this session. Confirm title, authors, and any number before citing.]**

- **Fire carbon-emissions context (CAMS/Copernicus):** Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service reported 2023 Canadian fires produced roughly **23% of global wildfire carbon emissions** for the year and set a Canadian record (~**327–355 Mt carbon** by late August; higher full-season; Wikipedia also cites a ~480 Mt carbon full-season figure) [VERIFIED via Wikipedia; **exact CAMS full-season tonnage UNVERIFIED — confirm on the CAMS site**].

## 3. Has the boreal "ever" burned at these scales? — paleofire & long-term context

*This is the section most prone to overclaim. Bottom line up front: the honest, defensible framing is **rate-of-change and regional exceedance of the Holocene range**, not an absolute "the most the boreal has ever burned." The paleofire record is heterogeneous and is a proxy, not a hectare count.*

**Charcoal is a proxy — not comparable to modern hectares.** Paleofire is reconstructed mainly from **charcoal accumulation in lake/bog sediments** (and fire-scarred trees / stand-age maps for recent centuries). These record fire *occurrence and biomass burned* near a site, integrated over time, with dating uncertainty. They **cannot be converted to national annual hectares burned.** Any comparison of a modern CIFFC hectare figure to a charcoal curve is apples-to-oranges and should be stated as such.

**Global/continental synthesis.** The **Global Charcoal Database** and syntheses (Power et al. 2008, *Climate Dynamics*; Marlon et al. 2008, *Nature Geoscience*; Marlon et al. 2013, *Quaternary Science Reviews*) show biomass burning tracking climate over millennia, with a notable global rise since ~1750 and especially the 20th century, but with large regional variation. **[Titles/authors correct to my knowledge; specific figures UNVERIFIED this session — confirm.]**

**Regional exceedance — the strongest "unprecedented" claim.** **Kelly et al. 2013, *PNAS* 110:13055–13060, "Recent burning of boreal forests exceeds fire regime limits of the past 10,000 years"** — from lake-sediment charcoal in the Alaskan Yukon Flats boreal — concluded that recent (late-20th/21st-century) burning **rates** in that region exceed the maximum of the last ~10,000 years. This supports a *regional* "unprecedented in the Holocene" statement for parts of the North American boreal. **[Title/journal correct to my knowledge; abstract text UNVERIFIED this session — publisher fetch blocked. Confirm before quoting.]** Note the claim is about a *specific region* and about *rates*, not "all of Canada."

**Regional heterogeneity — the crucial counterweight.** In parts of the **eastern** Canadian boreal, long-term reconstructions show fire activity **declined** over recent centuries (roughly since the Little Ice Age / into the 20th century), driven by moister conditions and vegetation/human factors. This body of work (Girardin and colleagues; Ali et al. 2012, *PNAS*, on Quebec boreal fire history; Hély, Bergeron) means a blanket "Canadian boreal fire is at an all-time high everywhere" is **false**. Fire has increased in the west/northwest while decreasing in parts of the east. **[Authors/theme correct to my knowledge; specific papers/numbers UNVERIFIED this session — confirm.]**

**Medieval Climate Anomaly (MCA).** Some records show **elevated fire during the MCA (~950–1250 CE)** in various Northern Hemisphere regions during warm/dry intervals — a reminder that warm past intervals also burned, and that current fire is best framed against the *full* Holocene envelope and its rate of change rather than against a single cool baseline. **[Established theme; specific regional figures UNVERIFIED — confirm.]**

**The 20th-century "fire deficit."** Active fire **suppression**, land-use change, and fragmentation drove burned area in many North American forests **below** what climate alone would predict for much of the 20th century — a "fire deficit" (Marlon et al. 2012, *PNAS* 109:E535–E543, "Long-term perspective on wildfires in the western USA"). This complicates "unprecedented" claims two ways: (i) part of the recent *increase* is a rebound from an artificially suppressed baseline, and (ii) the mid-20th-century low is not a "natural" reference. **[Title/journal correct to my knowledge; specific figures UNVERIFIED this session — confirm.]**

**Defensible synthesis for public use:**
- ✅ *"Recent burning rates in parts of the North American boreal exceed anything in the last ~10,000 years"* — defensible **regionally** (Kelly et al. 2013), not as a blanket national statement.
- ✅ *"The rate of increase in fire weather and area burned, and its climate attribution, is the robust story"* (WWA 2023; Jones et al. 2024).
- ⚠️ *"2023 is the most the Canadian boreal has ever burned"* — **overclaim.** It is the most in the ~60-year instrumental/CIFFC record, and extraordinary by any modern measure, but the multi-millennial record is heterogeneous, proxy-based, and not directly comparable.
- ⚠️ Always state: **charcoal ≠ hectares**; some eastern regions **declined**; a suppression-era **fire deficit** shapes the baseline.

## 4. Net assessment / honest bottom line

**What can be said with confidence:**
- Canada is warming ~2× (north ~3×) the global mean (CCCR 2019) [VERIFIED]. Warming raises VPD and fuel aridity, and fire responds non-linearly — so fire *potential* rises faster than temperature (Abatzoglou & Williams 2016; Seager et al. 2015).
- Fire weather, fire-season length, and area burned have **trended upward** over ~half a century, with regional variation (Hanes et al. 2019).
- **2023 was a real, extreme outlier** (~15–18 Mha, ~2.5× prior record) and climate change **substantially raised the odds and severity** of the fire weather that produced it (WWA 2023: ≥7× likelihood / ~50% intensity for cumulative severity; Jones et al. 2024: ~2.9–3.6× fire-weather likelihood) [VERIFIED].
- **2025 was the second-largest season on record** [VERIFIED].

**What cannot (yet) be said, or must be qualified:**
- Not "the most the boreal has ever burned" in an absolute, multi-millennial sense (§3).
- Attribution is cleanest for **fire *weather*** (FWI); the step from fire weather to **hectares** also involves ignition, suppression, fuels, and land management — WWA deliberately attributed the weather, not the area, and models may *understate* observed change.
- Trends in **blocking/heat-dome persistence** are an active, less-settled research area.

**Area burned vs. the air-quality crisis — do not conflate them.** Total hectares burned is the climate-linked metric. But whether a given city (e.g., during the 2026 smoke episodes) suffers hazardous air is governed by **where** fires burn, **plume injection height** (how high smoke is lofted), **wind trajectories/transport**, and **how many people live downwind** — i.e., meteorology and geography, not a monotonic function of total area burned. A season with fewer hectares but fires upwind of population centers under a transporting flow can produce **worse** urban air quality than a larger season that burns in remote areas or lofts smoke into the free troposphere and away from people. In public framing: *"climate change is increasing the fuel for these fires; where and when the smoke lands is a separate, weather-driven question."*
